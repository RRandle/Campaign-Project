Bob Childree throws hat in ring for RSA’s ERS Board of Control

RSA State retirees have a friend and advocate in Bob Childree

Bob Childree wants you to know that he cares about the success of the Retirement System of Alabama. A lot.

Childree is running for the ERS Board of Control in the upcoming RSA elections. And boy, does he have a lot to say about the state of the RSA, and the need for experienced financial experts like himself.

“The other gentleman running for the open board seat on the ERS Board of Control is a good man and a friend,” says Childree. “But he does not have the breadth and depth of experience with state financial matters that I have. And that’s not bragging - it’s just a fact.”

In fact, Childree does have quite a financial pedigree when it comes to understanding, fighting for, and helping retired state employees. He spent 22 years as State Comptroller for the State of Alabama. He’s the President-Elect of the Alabama Retired State Employees Association (ARSEA.) But mostly, it’s his experience with retirement programs OUTSIDE the state of Alabama that gives him well-needed insight and understanding of how state retirement systems can succeed - or fail - without the right safeguards and experienced leadership.

For example, in one of his many travels, he had the opportunity to speak to the state of New York’s Comptroller about their state pension fund, which has been around over 50 years. “I’ll never forget the advice he gave me when speaking about the peaks and valleys over time of pension funds. He said to me ‘Bob, our pension fund has been here over 50 years. 50 years from now, it’s still gonna be here. Why? Because peaks and valleys in unfunded liability are always going to happen. Ultimately, in the long term, sound pension plans will still be making payments to their retirees.’ That spoke volumes to me in terms of how you have to look at a pension fund over time to make wise decisions about administering it. If you have a knee-jerk reaction every time the markets take a bounce, you’ll make unwise long-term decisions.”

Why sitting on the RSA’s ERS Board of Control is so important to Bob Childree

Bob tells us, “it’s really simple. As the national scene and climate are shifting away from defined benefit pension plans, we need real strong leadership on the board to ensure the RSA is protected and given the ability to maintain the retirement system for payments which were promised and the retirees deserve.”
Bob is a strong man of principles, and this proved itself out time and time again as he served as Comptroller for the state under eight different governors. Explains Bob, “I served in a bipartisan role as Comptroller through all eight administrations. I treated everyone the same. Ultimately, every Finance Director I worked for under each administration was appreciative of the fact that I supported whatever administration was seated at the time. In many states, it would have been easy, even common, to replace me as Comptroller with each new administration. However, I worked with each administration to help accomplish their goals within the constraints of law and ethics, and I lasted 22 years, so I must have done something right!!”

“The other gentleman running for the open board seat on the ERS Board of Control is a good man and a friend,” says Childree. “But he does not have the breadth and depth of experience with state financial matters that I have. And that’s not bragging - it’s just a fact .”
So what’s the real crisis in Alabama’s pension plan?

Well if you ask Bob, he’ll tell you the proper way to begin the conversation is: do we actually have a pension crisis, or not? “Nationally there is a lot of press on how defined benefit pension plans are in a crisis state, with predictions that many state pension plans will have difficulty paying out everything that was promised to retirees.” He then goes on to explain that he feels this national narrative of a potential, widespread pension crisis was started because of two specific factors:

  1. Between 2000 and 2010, the USA had a horrible economic downtown (recession), and market values declined by 50%.
  2. At the same time, the Governmental Accounting Standards Board changed the way pension reporting was done. For many years, existing accounting principles did not require pension funds to disclose their unfunded liability in their financial statements. Then, it was determined by the Governmental Accounting Standards Board (GASB) that pension funds unfunded liabilities must be reported and proscribed their computation based on specific factors and standards. This resulted in liabilities that had not previously been reported, and the magnitude of the liabilities scared a lot of people.

As the economy declined, politicians called for a cut in state employment (“state government should be smaller!), which resulted in a loss of state jobs from upwards of 40,000 down now closer to 29,000. The result is obviously less money being paid into the retirement system now, because there are fewer employees to pay into it.

In Bob’s opinion, this is a very simplified explanation of what caused the perception of a “pension crisis.”

Says Bob, “but the truth is, as long as there are sound investment strategies made by people who fully understand long-term investing, and as long as the Legislature continues paying the total amount that our actuary has determined to be required for the state pension fund, our pension fund will be viable for the future. In states with bad investment strategies and where Legislatures are not fully funding the actuarial amount necessary is where states get into trouble.”

“The Legislature in Alabama has done the RIGHT thing by consistently funding the amount determined by the actuary and that combined with the solid investment strategy of RSA ensures that we are not in crisis. The decline in funding ratio was simply due to economic conditions of the US. The Alabama legislature has been doing its part, and I have every reason to believe that this will continue. It’s really just a matter of understanding that this is a multi-generational investment: very long-term, and over that time frame, you’ll see that you’ve had ups and down but essentially great gain in the investments of the funds. Overall it’s a sound fund.”

So what will Bob Childree do on RSA’S ERS Board?

“As the State Comptroller I did not allow politics, intimidation or threats to compromise my role as fiduciary of the citizen's checkbook. As your ERS Board member I will uphold the same standards as I work to ensure the fiscal viability of our well-earned defined benefit pension. Leadership is paramount to the success of the ERS and RSA and we have been fortunate to have great leadership on our ERS Board. Those who serve on the Board must serve with Honesty, Integrity and Commitment. I will continue to uphold these values as your ERS representative.”
